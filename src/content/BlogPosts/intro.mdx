### Chapter One: The settlement of Jews in Western Europe

From a glorious beginning rabbinic scholarship in northern France and south-western Germany came to an end with the flight of Asher ben Yechiel from Germany to his settling in Toledo Castille. To trace the life of this pivotal figure in the history of the Jews in Europe is the purpose of this study.

The earliest reference to Jews in Western Europe is from Suetonius mentioning them as the mourners at the cremation funeral of Julius Caesar. (1)

Despite the assertion of Graetz that the Jews of the Rhineland were the descendants of Jewish women and Roman legionnaires (2); the mitochondrial DNA of Ashkenazi Jews shows strong influence of non-eastern influence while the patrilinear is heavily influenced by Near-Eastern origin. (3) Nonetheless as Graetz asserts the first Jewish communities were founded during Roman domination in Worms and Mayence (Mainz). In the Roman colony of Cologne, Jews were entitled to their own jurisdiction down to the Middle Ages. “A non—Jewish plaintiff even though he were a priest, was obliged to bring his suit against a Jew before the Jewish judge (bishop of the Jews). (see note 2)

However, a misreading of a Talmudical passage by Carl Brilch cannot be sustained to place Cologne as mentioned in tractate Yevamot. (4)

The beginning of organized established Jewish communities in Northern France and the Rhineland in Germany begins with Charlamagne (Karl Der Grosse). On July 20, 802, Isaac a Jew who was sent by Charlemagne to Harun Al Rashid in Bagdad returned to Aachen. With the intent to enrich his kingdom by trade Jews were allowed to settle along the Rhine in Speyer, Worms and Mainz (In Hebrew Shum) as well as in Regensburg, Magdeburg and other locations. Besides being merchants Jews were engaged in agriculture and viniculture. The Kalonymus family from Rome and Lucca settled in Mainz where they established what was to be the fountainhead of Jewish learning in what was then Northern France and South-Eastern Germany. (5)

The first of a long line of Ashkenazi rabbinical authorities was Gershom ben Yehudah born in Metz 960-1028 and known as the “Light of the Exile.” Gershom set the pattern for Ashkenazim by forbidding in most instance polygamy, necessitating a wife’s consent to divorce and protecting the privacy of letters sent by postal agents. (6) The foremost Ashkenazic commentator on the Babylonian Talmud, Rabbi Solomon ben Yitzchak (Rashi) was a student of Gershom’s successors in Mainz and Worms. (7)

Before the first crusade the Jews of Germany lived in peace and security. In Speyer they even had a privilege granted by the Bishop Rudiger Huozman. All this changed with the decimations of the First Crusade which began the lachrymose history of German Jewry in the Middle Ages. (8)

Despite the persecutions in Germany of which there were many, Jewish Talmudical scholarship was enhanced by several luminaries. Of those one of the most prominent was Rabbi Eliezer ben Natan who not only in his own right achieved a place of prominence but was the progenitor of so many distinguished descendants.

![](../../../../images/Blog/eliezar b nathan family tree.png)

Eliezer married the daughter of Eliakim with whom he was a fellow student in France under Rashi.

The legal protection of Jews in Medieval Germany was first looked upon with compassion by Eike von Repgow of Anhalt (1180c-1233p) in his Sachsenspiegel. (10)

The Roman Church especially under Pope Innocent III and the Fourth Lateran Council of 1215 sought to ensure the unequal status of Jews in Christian Europe. (11)

In 1286 Emperor Rudolph I in a mandate made clear that Jews were to be now considered “Servi Camerae” or the German “Kammerknechtschaft.” What had its beginning in protection descended to servitude. (12)

The undisputed rabbinical leader of the remains of the empire of Charlemagne was Rabbi Meir of Rothenburg (c1215-2 May 1293) as so entitled by Rabbi Menachem Meiri (13).
